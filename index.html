Why Should the World Tell Me What to Wear?

Fashion is a form of art in and of itself, a way of self-expression, and a marker of identity. From the beginning of time, clothing has been used to both highlight and absorb culture, characterizing cultures and subcultures. The concept of fashion arguably comes from impression after all, since in the words of Immanuel Kant, “the human being has the natural tendency to compare his behavior to that of a more important person”. It is the pursuit of the new, the new ‘standard’ of beauty, as depicted in movies, television, magazines, news, and radio. No matter how much you try not to change your style, you are forever under the influence of media sources. Comparatively, it is the very basis for globalization and modernization, A.K.A. ‘the new norm’.

What is Globalization?

Globalization is used to define the relationship of international, national, state, and local as a form of worldwide interconnectedness. It takes its form through concepts like colonialism, imperialism, populism, cultural difference, and glocalization, as well as economically, politically, culturally, socially, and digitally. The world isn’t exactly unionizing solely through tradition and identity but hybridity and deterritorialization, which is spread through media, making connections.

Clothes and Tradition

Clothing is its own lens for globalization. Garments can represent religion, status, culture, and importantly, tradition. Long before deterritorialization, global textile and dye were connected across continents. For example, silk originated in the Henan Province in China, and spread as a form of currency through China and eventually all of the East from the Silk Road. The color blue also has roots in many cultures like Egypt, Italy, and many European countries as a color of wealth, mainly because early forms of blue were from Afghanistan using a stone known as lapis lazuli, deemed a rare and expensive material. These cultural roots in clothing are still persistent today, through traditions such as kente cloth, the national cloth of Ghana. Kente cloth is made by unraveling silks and fabrics from various different countries and is only worn on ceremonial occasions. The process of making indigo dye in Japan, known as Aizome, is used in Japanese fabrics and american jeans and still uses traditional methods of production. Traditional Tamil clothing, depending on the region, implements the silk sari. It is a long cloth draped around the waist and shoulder, paired with a blouse, a sign of auspiciousness. Its colors, fabrics, and blouse reflect centuries of colonialization and cultural exchange to its own form of regional identity.

Tradition to Trend

Fashion has progressed under the influence of culture and media. Through magazines, social media, news, and radio, fashion continues to hybridize, localize, and globalize. As mentioned, the color blue was once an expensive pigment and has now traveled globally through trade and has become a universal cultural symbol of labor for Japanese aizome, West African resist-dyeing (obroni wawa), and the American blue jeans. Silk is still used in many cultures and is a symbol of wealthy fashion to this day. Colonial governments introduced dress codes, which changed and invented traditions that fit Western ideas of ethnicity and culture. Post-colonialism, some nations reinvented traditional clothing through colonialism as a symbol of identity. When traditional clothing enters global markets, the history is overshadowed by media and commercial aesthetics. Things like kimono sleeves, kente patterns, or even gold jewelry are rebranded as trends. Things like second-hand clothing markets in Ghana create hybrid styles where Western clothing is adapted into traditional Ghanian garments. Ironically, some Western consumers wear ‘ethnic’ or ‘exotic’ garments as a form of hybridization. The whole Korean Wave is influenced by hanbok-inspired fashion and K-fashion aesthetics, advertised through media platforms, magazines, and pop stars, almost reinventing tradition.
